## Current Writing Plan

The current writing strategy is to build the paper around the multilevel concept of `pressability`.

### Summary

- Build the paper as a hybrid empirical HCI article with a controlled humanities/STS voice: dry, scientifically serious, and selectively laconically funny in framing, but never cute in methods, results, or claims.
- Ground the conceptual spine in `index.html`, especially its theoretical framework, six research divisions, and methodological framing.
- Ground the experimental rationale in `bigredbutton_placement.tex` plus the study design described by the user.
- Treat the paper's central object as a multilevel phenomenon called `pressability`: a symbolic, historical, technological, perceptual, social, and embodied relation between person and button.

### Section Architecture

Use the introduction to move from cultural overdetermination to experimental necessity.

Use the discussion to move from behavioral findings to a multilevel theory of pressability that remains valid whether results are positive, mixed, or null.

#### Introduction structure

1. Open with the big red button as a culturally saturated object: alarm, launch, emergency stop, meme, comic foreshadowing, and invitation to action.
2. Shift to STS and media history: buttons as discrete decision devices, delegation points, and artifacts of control that compress distributed judgment into a single event.
3. Add social and psychological layers: boredom, curiosity, ritualized participation, contribution, hesitation, and the social meaning of pressing in front of observers or instrumentation.
4. Add design and perceptual layers: redness, cylindrical protrusion, feature-singleton salience, reachability, peripersonal space, and button geometry as engineered invitation.
5. Identify the gap: prior literatures explain pieces of button salience, control, agency, biofeedback, and presence, but not the holistic phenomenon of voluntary big-red-button pressing under minimally directive conditions.
6. Present the study as a deliberately overdetermined paradigm: a button made culturally legible, perceptually salient, ergonomically reachable, and experimentally instrumented.
7. End with explicit contributions: operationalizing pressability, introducing an embodied-feedback red-button paradigm, and linking symbolic histories of control to empirical HCI measures of action, presence, and self-world coupling.

#### Discussion structure

1. Interpret primary behavior as pressability under agency, not mere compliance or task completion.
2. Interpret ECG and breathing manipulations as attempts to alter embodied coupling, whether by increasing action, modulating presence, or revealing limits of physiological identification.
3. Interpret the visible press counter as explicit participatory feedback and a micro-politics of contribution, where subjects become co-producers of the dataset.
4. Treat oneness and secondness as a novelty/repetition problem: first encounter cannot be fully repeated, and the paper should use that impossibility as a theoretical result rather than a mere nuisance variable.
5. Include three baked-in result branches:
   - Positive effect branch: embodied coupling increases pressability and/or subjective proximity to the button.
   - Mixed/null branch: symbolic and perceptual affordances may dominate physiological feedback, suggesting the red button is already overdetermined before embodiment is added.
   - Order-effect branch: novelty overwhelms condition effects, making first encounter itself the strongest explanatory variable.
6. Close with implications for HCI, STS, design research, neuroaesthetics, and ritualized interaction, then state limitations and inclusive boundaries clearly.

### Theme Matrix To Cover

- Cultural-symbolic layer: nuclear launch myth, alarms, emergency stop buttons, meme circulation, comedic foreshadowing, and the button as a demand to be pressed.
- Historical-technical/STS layer: emergence of buttons within industrial control, interface simplification, event-based decision offloading, and why buttons became preferable to levers or distributed monitoring in many settings.
- Social layer: pressing as participation, performance, contribution, contagion, and social coordination.
- Psychological layer: boredom, curiosity, urge, hesitation, novelty, repetition, and agency.
- Design/perceptual layer: red as warning and salience cue, cylindrical geometry, prominence, affordance, reach, and attentional capture.
- Embodied layer: ECG, breathing-linked lighting, physiological mirroring, presence, and peripersonal space.
- Complexity layer: interval dynamics, entropy, bursts, recurrence, and multiscale temporal structure as interpretive lenses for the paper's behavioral data.
- Inclusive layer: do not universalize Western red-button symbolism; explicitly account for cultural variance, handedness, reach differences, disability/access needs, prior VR familiarity, and the fact that "red means danger" is powerful but not globally or historically uniform.

## Study Summary for the Next Agent

The study places participants in a neutral grey VR room with a single large red button positioned at optimized arm's-length reach according to the placement rationale in `bigredbutton_placement.tex`. A reassuring audio track explicitly tells participants that they may press the button or not press it, whenever they want, however often they want, or never at all. This is meant to preserve agency and prevent the task from collapsing into compliance.

The experiment includes both explicit and implicit feedback. Explicitly, a counter above the button reports how many times the participant has pressed it, framing each action as visible and contributory. The conceptual logic here is participatory datafication: the participant can see themselves producing data and may feel that their pressing matters to the research process.

Implicitly, one condition uses live ECG from a Polar H10 connected through BLE to a Quest 3 headset, with R-R activity linked to a flashing red tone or pulse of the button. A control condition uses sham ECG generated with NeuroKit-like simulated dynamics. The core hypothesis is that embodied physiological coupling may make the button feel more like an extension of the self, thereby increasing connectedness, presence, and possibly press frequency.

Breathing derived from the Polar H10 is also used as a secondary feedback channel, with inhales brightening the environment and exhales dimming it. This should be treated as an embodied environmental coupling manipulation rather than a decorative effect.

The design is described as double blind across the live ECG and sham ECG conditions.

Primary and secondary post-trial measures include:

- presence
- felt peripersonal space between participant and button
- oneness
- secondness

Novelty is not just a nuisance variable here. The user explicitly frames first encounter as theoretically central. A participant can only encounter the button for the first time once, which means repeated-measures subjectivity is constrained by the irreversibility of first contact. The paper should treat that limitation as part of the phenomenon rather than only as a flaw in the design.
